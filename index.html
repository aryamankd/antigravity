Dictionary

A

Automatic

+

A mechanical watch that winds itself using a rotor — a semicircular weight that spins with the motion of your wrist. Also called “self-winding.”

Annual Calendar

+

A complication that displays day, date, and month, automatically accounting for months with 30 or 31 days. Only requires one manual correction per year (end of February).

Amplitude

+

The arc (in degrees) that the balance wheel swings through during each oscillation. A healthy mechanical watch typically has 250°–310°.

Appliqué

+

Hour markers or indices that are separately manufactured and then attached to the dial, rather than printed. Typically made of metal for a three-dimensional effect.

AR Coating

+

Anti-reflective coating applied to the crystal to reduce glare and improve legibility. Can be applied to one or both sides of the crystal.

B

Bezel

+

The ring surrounding the crystal on a watch case. Can be fixed or rotating, with markings for timing, GMT, tachymeter, or dive bezels.

Balance Wheel

+

The oscillating component at the heart of a mechanical movement that regulates timekeeping, typically vibrating at 28,800 times per hour (4 Hz).

Blued Hands

+

Watch hands heat-treated to approximately 300°C until they turn a deep, vivid blue. A hallmark of fine finishing in haute horlogerie.

Bracelet

+

A metal watch band made of individual links. Common styles include Oyster, Jubilee, President, and ladder bracelets.

C

Chronograph

+

A watch with a built-in stopwatch function, using sub-dials to track elapsed seconds, minutes, and sometimes hours. One of the most popular complications.

Caliber

+

The specific movement inside a watch, identified by number or name. “In-house” calibers designed and manufactured by the brand itself are considered a mark of prestige.

Complication

+

Any function beyond basic hours, minutes, and seconds — such as date, chronograph, GMT, moon phase, or power reserve. “Grand complications” combine multiple functions.

Crown

+

The knob on the side of a watch case used for winding and setting the time. Screw-down crowns thread into the case tube for enhanced water resistance.

COSC

+

Contrôle Officiel Suisse des Chronomètres — the official Swiss chronometer testing institute. Certified movements must keep time within -4/+6 seconds per day.

Caseback

+

The rear cover of a watch case. Can be solid (for durability) or transparent/exhibition (to showcase the movement). Secured by screws, snap-fit, or screw-down.

D

Dial

+

The face of the watch displaying the time. Can feature various textures like sunburst, guilloché, tapisserie, enamel, or meteorite.

Deployment Clasp

+

A folding buckle mechanism on a watch strap or bracelet that opens like a butterfly, keeping the watch securely on the wrist while making it easy to put on and remove.

Dress Watch

+

A thin, elegant timepiece designed for formal occasions. Typically features a simple dial, no complications beyond date, and fits under a shirt cuff.

E

Escapement

+

The mechanism that controls the release of energy from the mainspring to the gear train, one tooth at a time. The lever escapement is the most common type in mechanical watches.

Exhibition Caseback

+

A transparent caseback, usually sapphire crystal, that allows the wearer to view the movement and its finishing. Common in haute horlogerie pieces.

F

Flyback

+

A chronograph complication that allows the user to reset and restart the stopwatch with a single push, rather than the usual stop-reset-start sequence. Valued by pilots.

Fluted Bezel

+

A bezel with vertical ridges or grooves around its circumference. Originally functional (for grip when screwing), now primarily decorative. Iconic on the Rolex Datejust.

Foudroyante

+

A complication featuring a rapidly jumping hand that completes one revolution per second, dividing each second into fractions (typically quarters or eighths).

G

GMT

+

Greenwich Mean Time — a complication with an additional hand that tracks a second time zone on a 24-hour scale. Originally developed for Pan Am pilots.

Guilloché

+

An intricate, repetitive pattern engraved into the dial using a rose engine lathe. Creates a textured, light-catching surface. A hallmark of brands like Breguet.

Gasket

+

Rubber or synthetic seals placed at the crown, caseback, and crystal to prevent water and dust from entering the watch case.

H

Haute Horlogerie

+

French for “high watchmaking.” Refers to the highest level of watchmaking artistry, involving hand-finished movements, rare complications, and exceptional craftsmanship.

Hacking

+

A feature that stops the seconds hand when the crown is pulled out, allowing precise time-setting to the exact second. Standard in most modern mechanical watches.

Helium Escape Valve

+

A one-way valve found on saturation dive watches that releases helium gas that seeps into the case during deep-sea decompression, preventing crystal blow-off.

I

Incabloc

+

A widely used shock protection system for the balance wheel jewels in mechanical movements. Uses a spring-loaded setting that absorbs impacts, preventing jewel damage.

Index

+

The hour markers on a watch dial. Can be applied (raised metal), printed, or luminous. Styles include batons, dots, Roman numerals, and Arabic numerals.

J

Jewels

+

Synthetic ruby or sapphire bearings used at friction points in a movement to reduce wear. A standard automatic movement typically has 21–25 jewels.

Jumping Hour

+

A complication where the hour is displayed in a window (aperture) rather than on a hand, jumping instantaneously from one hour to the next.

K

Keyless Works

+

The mechanism that allows winding and time-setting via the crown, eliminating the need for a separate key. Standard in all modern watches since the mid-1800s.

L

Lug

+

The projections on a watch case that connect the strap or bracelet. Lug-to-lug distance determines how a watch wears on the wrist. Common styles include straight, curved, and hidden lugs.

Luminous Material

+

Photo-luminescent paint applied to hands and indices for low-light visibility. Modern materials include Super-LumiNova, Chromalight (Rolex), and tritium gas tubes.

M

Mainspring

+

The coiled spring inside the barrel that stores energy and powers a mechanical watch. When fully wound, it slowly uncoils to drive the gear train.

Minute Repeater

+

One of the most complex complications — chimes the time audibly on demand using tiny hammers and gongs inside the case. Hours, quarter-hours, and minutes each have a distinct tone.

Moon Phase

+

A complication that displays the current phase of the moon through an aperture on the dial, using a disc with two moons that completes one rotation every 59 days.

Movement

+

The complete mechanism (caliber) inside a watch that drives the hands and powers complications. Types include manual-wind, automatic, and quartz.

N

NATO Strap

+

A one-piece nylon strap that threads through both spring bars, originally issued to British Ministry of Defence personnel. Popular for its comfort, durability, and easy swapping.

O

Oscillation

+

One complete back-and-forth swing of the balance wheel. Measured in vibrations per hour (vph) — most modern movements oscillate at 28,800 vph (4 Hz).

Oyster Case

+

Rolex’s patented waterproof case design, introduced in 1926. Features a screw-down caseback, crown, and bezel, forming a hermetically sealed unit.

P

Power Reserve

+

The amount of time a mechanical watch can run when fully wound before stopping. Typically 40–80 hours for standard movements; some exceed 10 days.

Perpetual Calendar

+

A complication that automatically accounts for months of different lengths and leap years, requiring no manual date correction until the year 2100.

Perlage

+

A decorative finishing technique of overlapping circular patterns applied to movement plates, also called “snailing.” Purely aesthetic, indicating hand-finishing care.

Q

Quartz

+

A watch movement powered by a battery that sends current through a quartz crystal, causing it to vibrate at 32,768 Hz. Far more accurate than mechanical movements.

R

Regulator

+

A watch dial layout where hours, minutes, and seconds are each displayed on separate sub-dials or hands, rather than sharing a common center point.

Retrograde

+

A display where the hand travels along an arc rather than a full circle, then snaps back to the starting position. Often used for date or day-of-week displays.

Rotor

+

The semicircular weight in an automatic movement that spins with wrist motion to wind the mainspring. Can be full (360°) or micro-rotor (small, movement-integrated).

S

Sapphire Crystal

+

Synthetic corundum glass used to cover the dial. Scores 9 on the Mohs hardness scale (only diamond is harder), making it virtually scratch-proof.

Skeleton

+

A watch where the dial and/or movement plates are cut away to reveal the inner workings. Requires careful hand-finishing as all components are visible.

Spring Drive

+

Seiko’s proprietary hybrid movement that uses a mainspring for power but a quartz-regulated electromagnetic brake for timekeeping. Produces a perfectly smooth sweep.

Sub-dial

+

A smaller dial within the main dial, used to display additional information such as chronograph counters, small seconds, or calendar functions.

T

Tourbillon

+

A rotating cage that holds the escapement and balance wheel, completing one revolution per minute to counteract the effects of gravity on timekeeping accuracy.

Tachymeter

+

A scale on the bezel or dial used to measure speed over a known distance. Start the chronograph, stop at the mile/kilometer marker, and read speed off the scale.

Tritium

+

A radioactive hydrogen isotope formerly used in watch lume. Self-powered (no charging needed) but decays over ~12 years. Replaced by Super-LumiNova in modern watches.

U

Unidirectional Bezel

+

A rotating bezel that only turns counter-clockwise, used on dive watches. This safety feature ensures elapsed dive time can only increase if accidentally bumped.

V

Valjoux

+

A family of widely used Swiss chronograph movements made by ETA (Swatch Group). The Valjoux 7750 is one of the most common automatic chronograph calibers in the industry.

Vibrations Per Hour

+

The frequency of a movement’s balance wheel oscillation. Common rates: 21,600 vph (3 Hz), 28,800 vph (4 Hz), and 36,000 vph (5 Hz). Higher frequency generally means smoother seconds hand sweep.

W

Water Resistance

+

A watch’s ability to withstand water pressure, rated in meters or ATM. 30m = splash-proof, 100m = swimming, 200m+ = diving. Ratings assume static pressure, not real-world use.

Winding

+

The process of tensioning the mainspring to store energy. Can be done manually (turning the crown) or automatically (via a rotor responding to wrist movement).

Y

Yacht-Timer

+

A regatta countdown complication used in yacht racing. Counts down the minutes before a race start, typically 10, 5, or 1 minute, with visual or audible signals.

Z

Zaratsu Polishing

+

A mirror-finishing technique perfected by Seiko’s Grand Seiko division. The case is held against a rotating tin plate to achieve a perfectly flat, distortion-free mirror surface.

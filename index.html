The Collector's Glossary

Essential watch terminology from A to Z.

Automatic

+

A mechanical watch that winds itself using a rotor — a semicircular weight that spins with the motion of your wrist. Also called "self-winding." Most modern luxury watches use this mechanism.

Annual Calendar

+

A complication that displays day, date, and month, automatically accounting for months with 30 or 31 days. Only requires one manual correction per year (at the end of February).

Appliqué

+

Hour markers or indices that are separately manufactured and then attached to the dial, rather than printed. Typically made of metal and often found on higher-end watches.

Amplitude

+

The arc (in degrees) that the balance wheel swings through during each oscillation. A healthy mechanical watch typically has an amplitude between 250° and 310°. Low amplitude can indicate a service is needed.

AR Coating

+

Anti-reflective coating applied to the crystal to reduce glare and improve legibility. Can be applied to one or both sides. Double-sided AR gives the best clarity but is more prone to smudging.

Bezel

+

The ring surrounding the crystal on the front of the watch. Can be fixed or rotating, and often features markings for timing (dive bezels), tracking a second time zone (GMT), or measuring speed (tachymeter).

Balance Wheel

+

The oscillating component at the heart of a mechanical movement that regulates timekeeping. It swings back and forth at a precise rate (typically 28,800 vibrations per hour), dividing time into equal segments.

Blued Hands

+

Watch hands that have been heat-treated until they turn a deep blue color. True blued steel is heated to approximately 300°C. The technique is a hallmark of fine finishing and is prized in haute horlogerie.

Bracelet

+

A metal band attached to the watch case, made up of individual links. Common styles include Oyster (flat three-piece links), Jubilee (five-piece links), and President (semi-circular links).

Bridges

+

Metal plates within a movement that hold gears, springs, and other components in place. In high-end watchmaking, bridges are often decorated with Geneva stripes, perlage, or beveled edges (anglage).

Chronograph

+

A watch with a built-in stopwatch function, operated by pushers on the case side. Features sub-dials that track elapsed seconds, minutes, and sometimes hours. One of the most popular complications.

Caliber

+

The specific movement used in a watch, identified by a number or name. An "in-house" caliber is designed and manufactured by the brand itself — a mark of prestige. Example: Rolex Caliber 3235.

Complication

+

Any function on a watch beyond displaying hours, minutes, and seconds. Common complications include date, chronograph, GMT, moon phase, and power reserve. "Grand complications" combine multiple complex functions.

Crown

+

The small knob on the side of the watch used for winding and setting the time. A screw-down crown threads into the case for improved water resistance. Position determines function: pushed in, first click (date), pulled out (time).

COSC

+

Contrôle Officiel Suisse des Chronomètres — the official Swiss chronometer testing institute. A COSC-certified watch must keep time within -4/+6 seconds per day across multiple positions and temperatures over 15 days.
